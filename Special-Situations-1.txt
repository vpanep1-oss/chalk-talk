Chapter One: Transition Offense
Basic Philosophy
This is an extension of the half court offense.
● Make it positionless.
○ All positions in a transition offense are interchangeable.
● Use motion principles.
● Players need to push and attack up the floor.
● Great spacing, ball movement, and player movement are important.
Key Teaching Points
● Player who receives the inbounds must do three things
○ Get their back and butt to the sideline.
○ Peek down court after receiving the pass.
○ Catch the inbounds as far up the court as possible.
● Players should run down the court in the lane they are in.
● If a player is closest to the right lane, he/she should
sprint down the right lane.
● First three strides must be quick when filling the
lanes.
● Outside lanes need to get wide and look over their
inside shoulder for a pass.
● Person running down the middle lane is the rim
runner. The rim runner sprints straight down the
court to their basket.

Utah Transition
Pitch Ahead
● This drill focuses on sprinting up and down the floor.
● Players need to run early to get a layup.
● This drill also conditions players not to over dribble.
● This drill will focus on the half second outlet.
○ A half second outlet is when a player rebounds the ball and turns in the air.
This takes away the time to pivot.
○ Teach youth players to secure the rebound first and THEN pivot.
○ Only teach the half-second outlet to EXPERIENCED players.
● Younger players may need to take a dribble for
momentum before outletting.
● After three strides look for the ball.
● If the ball is thrown behind the player, the player
must stop, catch the ball with two hands, step
toward the basket, and rip the ball knee to knee.
● More experienced players can finish with a two foot
power layup, inside hand finish, goofy foot finish, or
a Euro step.
● Players 3rd-5th grade should finish with a regular
layup or two foot power layup.

Chapter Three: Transition Defense
Purpose
● Prevent offensive teams from getting easy baskets.
● Force the team to shoot over top of the defense.
○ Create a wall between the basket and the offensive player.
○ Must transition quickly to create contested shots.
Sprint in Transition
● Four seconds or less
○ Players should be able to sprint from the bottom block to the far side bottom
block in four seconds or less.
○ When they accomplish running the court in four seconds or less, they should
focus on having their head on a swivel.
■ Keeping your head on a swivel helps you locate the offense and the ball.
Transition Defense Keys
● Sprint Back.
● Keep your head on a swivel.
● Closest defender stops the ball.
○ Get the ball picked up as soon as possible.
● Push the ball toward the sideline.
● Take away the lane and then filter out.
● Players should not worry about the person they are guarding- stop the ball and
protect the basket.
○ Once the offense slows down and gets settled, players can switch to match up
with the person they were guarding.
○ The primary concern is no easy baskets in transition- correct matchups is a
secondary concern.

Tear Butt Transition Drill
Purpose of the Drill
● Players will be working on offensive and defensive transitioning
● Offensive players are on the baseline, and the
defensive players are facing them at the free throw
line extended.
● The coach tosses the ball to the offensive player who
immediately dribbles down the court in transition.
● The defender who was opposite must run and touch
the baseline without affecting the offensive player
before transitioning to defense.
● Coaches can adapt the drill to fit any number of players.
○ This can be 5v5, 4v4, or even 3v3.
● The coach can also call out another player's name which forces that player to run
and touch the baseline as well as the person who was opposite the player who
received the ball.
○ The coach can make it 5v4 ,5v3, or 5v2.
● The offense has an advantage and should be in attack mode.
○ If there are no scoring opportunities in transition, flow straight into motion
offense.
● Transition defense should focus on stopping the ball and allowing no layups.
● Coaches can alternate who begins on offense.
● This drill can be continuous.
○ The defense can rebound the ball and transition to offense.
○ The defense can inbound the ball after a made shot and transition to offense.

Chapter Five: Press Break
Basic Philosophy
● Attack the defense.
● Space the floor.
● Move the ball.
● Avoid catching the ball, or dribbling into,
the coffin corners.
● Being in the coffin corners allows for the
defense to trap easily.
● Running the baseline shifts the defense
and helps break down pressure.
Keys to Breaking the Press
● Pause for poise/slow down.
○ Kids will get nervous and speed up causing turnovers. Get them to slow down
and relax.
● Play in the present.
○ Forget about the mistakes that have been made. Focus on what is happening
now.
● No live turnovers.
○ These lead to easier transition buckets for the opposing team.
● Look before you dribble.
○ Players who keep their head up are less likely to dribble into a trap.
● Beat the trap.
○ Use moves to split the trap.
● Attack the basket.
17
Breakthrough Basketball
○ It is not enough just to get the ball up the floor. Attack the basket and try to
get an easy bucket. If you have an advantage in numbers, use it.
● Keep good spacing.
○ By being close together, fewer defenders can guard more people.
● Reverse the ball.
○ This causes the defense to shift which creates gaps.
○ Reversing the ball will make beating the press much easier.
● Step and meet passes.
○ This shortens the pass. It is less likely to be stolen.
Press Breaker
● This press break can be used against man or zone pressure.
● Players are set in these positions to break the press.

Chapter Six: Full Court Pressure
Key Tip to Remember
● Focus on half-court defense before emphasizing full court pressure.
● If a team has good ball handling and is good off the bounce, using full court pressure
may not be a good idea.
○ If the opposing team continually exposes the full court pressure, get out of it.
● It is the same as man-to-man principles.
● Pressing can be an effective way of speeding up the tempo of an opponent.
● Pressing can expose a defense if the defenders do not sprint back and match up to
get even numbers.
Basic Principles
● Faceguard the offensive players when they are close to the ball getting inbounded.
○ The goal is to deny the offensive players the ball.
● Keep the offensive players in front.
● Players in the back of the press should be using a ¾ defensive position. They should
be on the side of the offensive player.
● If the ball gets past the defender, the defender must sprint back to get even or ahead
of the ball.
Setting a Good Trap
● Feet to feet with teammate.
○ Prevent gaps offensive players can step through.
● Hands are UP and ACTIVE.
○ Prevent passing lanes and get deflections.

Chapter Seven: Zone Offense
Basic Philosophy
1. Beat the defense down the floor.
○ Defenders are running to spots. If the offense is quick enough, defenders may
not be set up which allows for easier baskets.
2. Play inside out.
○ Offensive players playing down low can seal the defenders creating easier
passing lanes.
○ Once the inside player receives the ball, the defense is forced to collapse.
○ Too many zone offenses just reverse the ball around the outside of the zone
and take outside shots.
3. Make ball reservals.
○ This forces the defense to shift.
○ If a defender is slow to shift, gaps are created allowing the offensive player to
penetrate.
4. Use pass fakes.
○ This also creates gaps in the zone defense.
○ Defenders will believe the pass is going to be made, so they will shift adjusting
to the offensive players.
○ Pass fakes must be convincing or they will not be effective.

5. For triangles on offense.
● By creating triangles, the offense is
overloaded on one side.
● The defense then has to decide
which person to guard.
6. Force decisions by the defense.
○ Put pressure on the defense to decide which offensive players to guard.
○ When someone penetrates, the defender has to choose between shifting to
help defend or remain in a specific zone area.
7. Split the defense and attack gaps.
● When an offensive player attacks a
gap, two defenders will be pulled to
guard the offensive player.
● When the defense collapses, the
offensive player can kick to an open
teammate.

5 Cut vs. 2-3 Zone
Basic Principles
● Play in the gaps.
○ Offensive players should shade to the gaps creating the triangles.
● Ball reversals.
● Ball should go inside out before a shot is taken.
● After a pass is made, the passer should make a banana cut to the basket before
cutting all the way through.
● Cuts must be hard to be effective.
● Getting the ball inside forces the defense to collapse which creates kick out
opportunities for open outside shots.
● Penetration creates easy scoring opportunities.
● Cutters put pressure on the defense and move the defenders.
● If the defense isn’t guarding the ball, the player with the ball should attack the
basket.
● Fake a pass to make a pass.
● Enter the ball to the player in the paint if they are open.
● Player who is positioned in the short
corner should have butt and back to
the baseline.
● This player can be a threat to catch
and shoot, step through and
penetrate, or pass.
● Short corner can play behind the
baseline defenders as well.

Guidelines for Youth Inbounds Plays
● Keep them simple.
○ Any number that starts with “1” is the same play
○ Opposing coaches will think it is a different play because it is a different
number.
● Don’t spend too much time on them in practice.
○ Focus time on skill work, not plays.
Blob #1
● The coach will call out the number
for the inbounds play.
● The player inbounding the ball will
also call out the number while
raising the hand showing which side
screens first.
